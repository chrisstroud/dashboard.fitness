## 1. Thyroid Function at 40

**Evidence: Strong for testing. Moderate for optimal ranges vs. "normal."**

The thyroid controls metabolic rate, energy, body composition, mood, and cognitive function. It's the master regulator of how fast your body runs. At 40, subclinical thyroid dysfunction becomes increasingly common and is frequently missed by conventional screening.

### What to Test

| Marker | What It Tells You | Conventional Range | Optimal Range |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **TSH** | Pituitary signal to thyroid — higher = thyroid underperforming | 0.5–4.5 mIU/L | **0.5–2.0 mIU/L** |
| **Free T4** | Inactive storage hormone — what the thyroid produces | 0.8–1.8 ng/dL | **1.0–1.5 ng/dL** (mid-range) |
| **Free T3** | Active hormone — what cells actually use | 2.3–4.2 pg/mL | **3.0–4.2 pg/mL** (upper half) |
| **Reverse T3** | Inactive T3 — rises under stress, illness, caloric deficit | 8–25 ng/dL | **<15 ng/dL** |
| **TPO Antibodies** | Autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's) | <35 IU/mL | **<10 IU/mL** |
| **Thyroglobulin Ab** | Second autoimmune marker | <1 IU/mL | **<1 IU/mL** |

A 2024 meta-analysis found that TSH in the 60th–80th percentile of "normal" (roughly 2.0–3.5 mIU/L) was already associated with increased cardiovascular mortality. The functional medicine range of 0.5–2.0 aligns with this data. Attia recommends looking beyond TSH alone — the ratio of Free T3 to Reverse T3 reveals how efficiently you're converting storage hormone to active hormone.

> **Attia's framing:** "It's less about a model and more about a nuanced way of understanding the physiology behind the conversion from T4 to T3 and rT3." The enzymes (deiodinases) that make the conversion are the key — not just the numbers in isolation.

### How Training and Nutrition Affect Thyroid

- **Caloric deficit** downregulates T4-to-T3 conversion and increases Reverse T3 (the body's energy-conservation mode). This is called "low T3 syndrome" or euthyroid sick syndrome — your thyroid is fine, but your body is throttling output to save fuel. *Relevant to the current lean-out meso (March–April).*
- **Overtraining** can mimic caloric deficit effects on thyroid — high training volume + insufficient calories = suppressed T3
- **Selenium** is required for the deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3. Two Brazil nuts daily covers it. Already in current nutrition (Brazil nuts in last meal — see [Testosterone Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md))
- **Zinc** supports thyroid hormone synthesis. Already supplemented (15mg picolinate — see [Daily Routine](../Daily%20Routine.md))
- **Iodine** is the raw material for thyroid hormones. Most Americans get enough from iodized salt, dairy, and seafood. Supplementation beyond a multivitamin is rarely needed and can be harmful

### Subclinical Hypothyroidism in Active Men

TSH between 2.5–4.5 with normal Free T4 — technically "normal" but may cause fatigue, difficulty losing fat, cold intolerance, brain fog, and reduced recovery. A 2022 systematic review found that exercise (3–4x/week aerobic + resistance) improved TSH, symptoms, and quality of life in subclinical hypothyroidism without medication. Your current training load is likely protective — but only if calories are adequate.

**Key insight:** If you hit a wall on the lean-out — persistent fatigue, unexpected weight plateau, cold hands — get thyroid labs before blaming discipline. Caloric restriction + high training volume is the exact recipe for low T3 syndrome.

### Nutrient Support for Thyroid

| Nutrient | Role | Current Status |
|----------|------|---------------|
| Selenium | T4→T3 conversion | Covered (Brazil nuts in diet) |
| Zinc | Thyroid hormone synthesis | Covered (15mg picolinate daily) |
| Iodine | Raw material for T3/T4 | Likely covered via diet + multivitamin |
| Iron | Required for thyroid peroxidase enzyme | Unknown — test ferritin with bloodwork |
| Vitamin D | Thyroid autoimmune protection | Covered (5,000 IU D3 daily) |

## 2. Insulin Sensitivity & Glucose Management

**Evidence: Strong. This is Attia's primary focus area — he considers metabolic dysfunction the foundational disease.**

### Why It Matters at 40

Insulin resistance is the upstream driver of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's (Attia calls it "type 3 diabetes"), and accelerated aging. It develops silently over 10–20 years. Fasting glucose and HbA1c are late signals — by the time they're abnormal, you've been insulin resistant for years.

### What to Test

| Marker | What It Tells You | "Normal" Range | Attia's Optimal |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Fasting Insulin** | How hard your pancreas works to keep glucose normal | 2–20 uIU/mL | **<6 uIU/mL** |
| **Fasting Glucose** | Blood sugar after overnight fast | 70–100 mg/dL | **<90 mg/dL** |
| **HbA1c** | 3-month glucose average | <5.7% | **<5.3%** |
| **HOMA-IR** | Insulin resistance score (glucose x insulin / 405) | <2.5 | **<1.0** |
| **Triglyceride:HDL ratio** | Proxy for insulin resistance | — | **<1.5** (ideally <1.0) |

> **Attia's key insight:** "Hyperinsulinemia on an oral glucose tolerance test, even in the presence of normoglycemia, is the canary in the coal mine." Your glucose can look perfect while your insulin is working overtime to keep it there. Fasting insulin is the marker most doctors don't order — and it's the most important metabolic number you can get.

### The CGM Experiment — Is It Worth It?

**Evidence: Moderate for non-diabetics. Strong for behavioral change.**

Attia has all his patients wear a CGM for 1–2 months, even healthy ones. Here's what it teaches:

- Which foods spike *your* glucose (individual variation is enormous — rice may spike you but not someone else)
- How meal timing, exercise, and sleep affect your glucose curve
- Target metrics: average glucose **<100 mg/dL**, standard deviation **<15 mg/dL**, peak post-meal glucose **<140 mg/dL** (ideally <120)
- Walking 10–15 minutes after meals dramatically flattens glucose spikes
- After 1–2 months, most people have learned enough to stop wearing it

**Recommendation:** Worth a 2-week experiment. Dexcom G7 or Stelo (over-the-counter, no prescription needed, ~$90/month). Wear it through a normal training week — you'll learn your glucose response to your actual meals, not theoretical ones.

**What your current protocol likely does for insulin sensitivity:**
- Strength training 4x/week — **the single most powerful insulin-sensitizing intervention** (Attia ranks it #1). Muscle is the primary glucose sink in the body
- Zone 2 cardio 2x/week — increases mitochondrial density, improves fat oxidation, reduces insulin demand
- Discontinuing IF — good call for performance; IF improves insulin sensitivity acutely but can suppress T in trained men (already noted in [T Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md))
- High protein, whole foods, no processed sugar — reduces glycemic load
- **Net assessment: your current protocol is likely very insulin-sensitive.** Bloodwork will confirm.

### Attia's Five Tools for Glucose Management

1. **Exercise** — strength training is #1, Zone 2 is #2
2. **Nutrition** — reduce refined carbs, increase fiber, eat protein first
3. **Sleep** — one night of poor sleep reduces insulin sensitivity 25–40%
4. **Stress management** — cortisol directly raises blood glucose
5. **Pharmacology** — metformin, acarbose, GLP-1 agonists (not relevant until there's a problem)

## 3. Cortisol Rhythm

**Evidence: Strong for diurnal pattern. Strong for ashwagandha. Moderate for optimization protocols.**

### The Healthy Cortisol Pattern

Cortisol follows a strict 24-hour rhythm. When it's working:

- **Lowest point:** ~midnight (during deep sleep)
- **Rising:** 3–5 AM
- **Peak:** within 30–45 minutes of waking (the Cortisol Awakening Response, or CAR)
- **Gradual decline:** through afternoon and evening
- **Low by 9 PM:** enables melatonin release and sleep onset

When this rhythm breaks — from chronic stress, overtraining, sleep deprivation, or shift work — everything downstream suffers: testosterone drops, thyroid slows, insulin sensitivity worsens, immune function degrades, and body fat accumulates (especially visceral fat).

### How to Know If It's Dysregulated

| Sign | What It Suggests |
|------|-----------------|
| Can't wake up / need an alarm | Blunted morning cortisol peak |
| Wired at night, can't wind down | Elevated evening cortisol |
| Afternoon crash (2–4 PM) | Cortisol dropping too fast |
| Persistent fatigue despite sleep | Flat cortisol curve (no peak, no trough) |
| Frequent illness | Immune suppression from chronic cortisol |
| Gaining belly fat despite training | Cortisol-driven visceral fat storage |

**Testing:** DUTCH test (dried urine, 4-point cortisol + metabolites) is the gold standard for mapping your full diurnal curve. Single-point serum cortisol is nearly useless. Cost: $200–400.

### What Your Current Stack Is Doing

| Intervention | Effect on Cortisol | Status |
|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Ashwagandha KSM-66 600mg** | Reduces cortisol 14.5–27.9% | Taking nightly |
| **Magnesium Glycinate 400mg** | Reduces HPA axis reactivity | Taking nightly |
| **L-Theanine 200mg** | Promotes alpha brain waves, reduces cortisol | Taking nightly |
| **8 hours sleep (9 PM–5 AM)** | Enables full cortisol reset | Dialed |
| **Morning sunlight** | Sets cortisol awakening response | Dialed |
| **Cyclic sighing** | Acute cortisol reduction (2023 Stanford RCT) | In T Protocol |

**Assessment: Your cortisol management stack is strong.** The combination of ashwagandha (evening cortisol blunter), mag glycinate (HPA axis calmer), L-theanine (anxiolytic), morning sunlight (CAR setter), and 8 hours of sleep is a near-optimal natural stack.

### Huberman's Ashwagandha Cycling Protocol

Huberman recommends **two weeks on, two weeks off** for ashwagandha. His reasoning: chronic cortisol suppression can blunt beneficial exercise-induced cortisol spikes and potentially affect other hormone pathways.

> **Key point:** Some cortisol is good. The acute cortisol spike from training drives adaptation. You don't want to eliminate cortisol — you want a sharp morning peak, a strong exercise response, and a clean drop-off by evening.

Attia takes 600mg ashwagandha at bedtime and hasn't publicly recommended cycling. The cycling evidence is theoretical, not from RCTs. **Practical approach:** Try 4 weeks on / 1 week off and see if you notice a difference. If not, continuous use is fine.

### What Else Could Be Optimized

- **Delay caffeine 60–90 minutes after waking** (Huberman) — allows the natural cortisol peak to do its job. Caffeine on top of peak cortisol = jittery, then crash. *Check current protocol: coffee is at 5 AM immediately after waking.*
- **Starchy carbs at dinner** — lowers evening cortisol. Sweet potato or rice at the last meal (already in diet) is doing this
- **Phosphatidylserine 400mg** — blunts evening cortisol. Already on the "considering" list in [Daily Routine](../Daily%20Routine.md). Evidence is moderate. Add it if evening wind-down is difficult
- **No Wim Hof breathing at night** — already noted in [T Research](Testosterone%20Research.md). It's a cortisol spike

## 4. Growth Hormone

**Evidence: Strong for sleep-mediated GH. Moderate for sauna. Moderate for sermorelin. Weak for most "natural GH boosters."**

### GH Basics at 40

Growth hormone secretion declines ~14% per decade after 30. By 40, you're producing roughly 60–70% of what you did at 25. GH drives fat metabolism, tissue repair, recovery, sleep quality, and skin/hair health. It works through IGF-1 (see Section 8).

### Natural GH Optimization

**Sleep (the biggest lever):**

~70–75% of daily GH is released during deep (Stage 3/4 NREM) sleep, concentrated in the first 1–2 hours after falling asleep. This is why the T Protocol emphasizes finishing training 2–3 hours before bed and keeping the bedroom at 65–68 degrees.

**What suppresses sleep-mediated GH:**
- Eating within 2 hours of bed (insulin suppresses GH release)
- Alcohol (destroys deep sleep architecture)
- Late-night screen exposure (delays sleep onset, shortens deep sleep)
- Elevated evening cortisol

> **Your current protocol protects this.** Stop eating by 7 PM (3 hours before sleep), no alcohol, screens off by 8 PM, bedroom optimized. This is the highest-ROI GH strategy.

**Sauna:**

| Protocol | GH Increase | Study Details |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| 2x 20 min at 176 degrees F, 30 min cool-down between | **2x baseline** | Single session |
| 2x 20 min at 212 degrees F, 30 min cool-down between | **5x baseline** | Single session |
| 1 hour, 2x/day for 7 days at 176 degrees F | **16x baseline** (by day 3) | Extreme protocol, not practical |

**Key guidelines for GH-focused sauna use:**
- Use infrequently for GH (once per week or less) — frequent use causes adaptation and diminished GH response
- Semi-fasted state (2–3 hours after last meal) — low insulin = stronger GH release
- Temperature: 176–212 degrees F (80–100 degrees C)
- Duration: 20–30 minutes minimum
- Time it 4+ hours after lifting (already noted in T Research — immediate post-workout sauna blunts hormonal response)
- For cardiovascular benefits, frequent use (4–7x/week) is better — different goal, different protocol

> **Note on Ethos:** Sauna is listed in the "California Posture Daddy" lifestyle layer. When access is available, use weekly for GH (infrequent, fasted, high-heat) and more frequently for cardiovascular/recovery (moderate heat, any time).

**Fasting and GH:**

Extended fasting (24–72 hours) dramatically increases GH — up to 5x by day 2. This is the body's muscle-preservation mechanism during starvation. However, IF was discontinued to support training performance and post-workout protein timing. This was the right call — the T and performance costs of fasting outweigh the GH benefit in a trained man.

**Exercise:**

High-intensity resistance training and HIIT both spike GH acutely. Already covered by current training program.

### Sermorelin — Honest Evidence Assessment

*Already covered in [T Research](Testosterone%20Research.md). Expanded here.*

**What it is:** Synthetic GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone) that stimulates your pituitary to release GH in natural pulses. Still legal to compound (unlike CJC-1295/Ipamorelin, banned August 2024).

**Evidence: Moderate.**

| Claim | Evidence | Reality |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Increases GH secretion | Strong (pharmacological studies) | Yes, measurably raises GH and IGF-1 |
| Improves sleep quality | Moderate (subjective reports + some objective data) | Likely — GH and deep sleep are bidirectional |
| Fat loss | Moderate (small studies) | Modest — don't expect dramatic recomp |
| Muscle gain | Weak in healthy trained men | Minimal on top of good training + nutrition |
| Recovery improvement | Moderate (anecdotal + mechanism) | Plausible — GH drives tissue repair |
| Anti-aging | Weak (no long-term outcome data) | Theoretical |

**The honest assessment:** Sermorelin is a real compound with real pharmacology. It will raise your GH. The question is whether that matters enough to justify $150–225/month, nightly subcutaneous injections, and unknown long-term effects — when your sleep, sauna, training, and nutrition are already optimizing GH through the same pathway.

**When it might make sense:**
- After bloodwork shows low IGF-1 (below 120 ng/mL)
- If sleep quality is poor despite optimizing everything else
- If recovery is clearly limiting training progress after 6+ months of dialing in lifestyle

**When it doesn't make sense:**
- Before getting baseline bloodwork
- Before maximizing sleep, sauna, and training
- As a substitute for lifestyle optimization

## 5. DHEA-S

**Evidence: Moderate for diagnostic value. Weak for supplementation in healthy men.**

### What It Tells You

DHEA-S (dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate) is the most abundant steroid hormone in the body and the primary output of the adrenal glands. It's the precursor to both testosterone and estrogen. It serves as a biomarker of adrenal reserve — how much juice the adrenals have left.

### Age-Related Decline

DHEA peaks in your mid-20s and drops ~10% per decade. By 40, you're at roughly 60–70% of peak. By 70, you're at 10–20%. This decline is called "adrenopause" and parallels the decline in testosterone, though they decline through different mechanisms.

### Optimal Ranges

| Age | DHEA-S Range (mcg/dL) | Optimal |
|-----|----------------------|---------|
| 30–39 | 120–520 | **250–450** |
| 40–49 | 95–530 | **200–400** |

### What Low DHEA-S Means

- Adrenal fatigue (more accurately: HPA axis dysfunction)
- Chronic stress depleting adrenal output
- Overtraining
- Poor sleep
- Accelerated aging

### Should You Supplement DHEA?

**Evidence: Weak for healthy men with adequate levels.**

- Harvard Health (2024): "There's little evidence to support anti-aging claims" for DHEA supplementation
- A 2025 Mendelian randomization study found observational associations between DHEA-S and lifespan in men, but no causal role has been established
- Supplemental DHEA can convert to estrogen via aromatase — potentially raising estradiol in men with higher body fat
- DHEA may slightly increase prostate cancer risk markers
- Quality control of OTC DHEA supplements is poor

**Recommendation:** Test DHEA-S as part of baseline bloodwork. If it's in the lower quartile (<200 mcg/dL at age 40) with symptoms of fatigue and low recovery, discuss with a physician. Do not self-supplement — the downstream conversion to estrogen is unpredictable. If lifestyle optimization (sleep, stress management, training) doesn't improve it, a compounding pharmacy can provide pharmaceutical-grade DHEA at 25–50mg under medical supervision.

> **Cross-reference:** DHEA-S is already on the bloodwork template (see [template-bloodwork](../health/template-bloodwork.md)). It's listed in the T Protocol's optional bloodwork panel.

## 6. Estrogen Management in Men

**Evidence: Strong for the aromatase-body fat connection. Moderate for natural management. Strong for "don't crash your estrogen."**

*Expands on the aromatase section in [T Research](Testosterone%20Research.md).*

### The Basics

Men need estrogen. Estradiol (E2) is essential for bone density, cardiovascular health, cognitive function, libido, and joint health. The goal is not to eliminate estrogen — it's to keep it in balance with testosterone.

### Aromatase: The Conversion Engine

Aromatase is an enzyme concentrated in fat tissue that converts testosterone to estradiol. More body fat = more aromatase = more T-to-E2 conversion = lower T, higher E2.

> Already covered in T Research: body fat 8–15% is the sweet spot. Above 15%, aromatase activity rises meaningfully. Above 25%, clinically significant impacts.

### Optimal Estradiol Range for Men

| Estradiol Level | Assessment |
|----------------|------------|
| <15 pg/mL | **Too low.** Joint pain, bone loss, cognitive decline, mood disorders, low libido |
| 15–20 pg/mL | Low-normal. Monitor for bone and joint symptoms |
| **20–35 pg/mL** | **Optimal range.** Sweet spot for most men |
| 35–50 pg/mL | High-normal. Watch for symptoms |
| >50 pg/mL | **Too high.** Water retention, mood swings, gynecomastia risk, reduced libido |

### Symptoms of Imbalance

| High Estradiol | Low Estradiol |
|---------------|---------------|
| Water retention / bloating | Joint pain / cracking |
| Mood swings / irritability | Dry skin |
| Gynecomastia (breast tissue) | Bone loss (accelerates below 20 pg/mL) |
| Reduced libido | Cognitive fog / poor memory |
| Increased body fat | Depression / anxiety |
| Fatigue | Low libido (yes, both directions) |

> **Critical point:** Low estradiol is as bad as — or worse than — high estradiol. This is why aromatase inhibitors (AIs) like anastrozole are falling out of favor even in TRT protocols. Crashing estrogen has real consequences: bone loss, joint destruction, cognitive decline.

### Natural Estrogen Management

| Approach | Mechanism | Evidence | Notes |
|----------|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Reduce body fat to 10–15%** | Less aromatase enzyme | Strong | The primary lever. Already in current meso goal |
| **Cruciferous vegetables** | Contains DIM + I3C precursors | Moderate | Already in diet (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts) |
| **DIM supplement (100–300mg)** | Shifts estrogen metabolism toward protective metabolites | Moderate | Does NOT lower total estradiol — changes the ratio of metabolites. Gentler than an AI |
| **Zinc (15–30mg)** | Mild aromatase inhibition | Moderate | Already supplemented |
| **Reduce alcohol** | Alcohol increases aromatase activity | Strong | Already eliminated |
| **Reduce visceral fat** | Visceral fat has highest aromatase concentration | Strong | Zone 2 cardio uniquely targets this |

**Recommendation:** Don't take a DIM supplement or any AI without bloodwork showing elevated estradiol. Your current approach — clean diet with cruciferous vegetables, zinc supplementation, alcohol elimination, active body fat reduction, and Zone 2 cardio — is the evidence-based natural estrogen management protocol. Get baseline E2 and make decisions from data.

## 7. The HPA Axis — The Master Switch

**Evidence: Strong. This is the most interconnected and most abused system in the body.**

### What It Is

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is the body's central stress-response system. When stress is perceived:

1. **Hypothalamus** releases CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
2. **Pituitary** releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
3. **Adrenal glands** release cortisol

Cortisol then feeds back to suppress CRH and ACTH — a negative feedback loop that turns itself off. When this loop is chronically activated, it breaks.

### How Overtraining Breaks the HPA Axis

The EROS study (Endocrine and Metabolic Responses on Overtraining Syndrome) found that overtrained athletes show:

- Blunted cortisol response to exercise (the adrenals stop responding)
- Blunted GH response to exercise
- Blunted testosterone response to exercise
- The entire hormonal response to training becomes flat

This is not "adrenal fatigue" (a term without clinical validation). It's HPA axis dysfunction — the brain stops sending the signals because the system has been overwhelmed.

### The Hormonal Cascade of Chronic Stress

```
Chronic stress / overtraining / sleep deprivation
        ↓
HPA axis chronically activated
        ↓
Cortisol stays elevated → suppresses GnRH
        ↓
LH and FSH decrease → testosterone drops
        ↓
DHEA-S depleted → less precursor for T
        ↓
Thyroid downregulates (T3 drops, Reverse T3 rises)
        ↓
Insulin resistance increases (cortisol raises blood glucose)
        ↓
GH secretion blunted (elevated cortisol disrupts deep sleep)
        ↓
Prolactin may rise (stress-induced)
        ↓
Recovery collapses → performance declines → more stress → repeat
```

### The Testosterone-to-Cortisol Ratio

A healthy T:C ratio is above 30:1. Below this, cortisol is winning — recovery is impaired, muscle protein synthesis is blunted, and fat storage is favored. Endurance athletes who train >60 miles/week often show T levels comparable to hypogonadal men.

**Your current risk:** 4x/week strength + 2x cardio is a sustainable load for most trained men. The risk comes from *stacking stressors* — high training volume + caloric deficit (current lean-out meso) + life stress + insufficient recovery. Monitor overtraining warning signs from the [T Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md):
- Strength declining 2+ weeks
- Persistent fatigue
- Resting heart rate up 5–10 BPM
- Loss of morning erections

### How Sleep Deprivation Specifically Damages the HPA Axis

- One week of 5 hours/night → testosterone drops 10–15%
- Chronic sleep debt prevents the normal cortisol nadir (midnight low point)
- Disrupts the cortisol awakening response → morning grogginess
- Erodes the diurnal rhythm over months
- Elevated evening cortisol blocks melatonin → worse sleep → vicious cycle

> **Your 8-hour sleep window (9 PM–5 AM) is the single most protective factor for HPA axis health.** Guard it above all else.

## 8. IGF-1 — The Longevity Paradox

**Evidence: Strong for the paradox. Moderate for practical targets.**

### What IGF-1 Is

Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 is the downstream effector of growth hormone. When GH hits the liver, it produces IGF-1, which then drives muscle protein synthesis, bone growth, tissue repair, and cell proliferation throughout the body. IGF-1 is the measurable blood marker for GH activity — GH itself is too pulsatile to test reliably.

### The Paradox

This is the central tension in longevity science:

| High IGF-1 | Low IGF-1 |
|-----------|-----------|
| More muscle mass | Less muscle mass |
| Better recovery | Slower recovery |
| Stronger bones | Higher fracture risk |
| Increased cancer risk | Reduced cancer risk |
| Accelerated aging at cellular level | Slower cellular aging |
| Better function *now* | Longer lifespan *later* |

Attia and Patrick have both discussed this extensively. People with Laron syndrome (IGF-1 deficiency) are virtually immune to cancer and diabetes — but they're small and physically limited. Centenarian populations tend to have lower IGF-1. But muscle mass is the strongest predictor of all-cause mortality in older adults.

> **Attia's framing:** He wants IGF-1 lower from the neck down (cancer/cardiovascular protection) and higher above the neck (neuroprotection against Alzheimer's). This is partially achievable through nutrition and exercise — but it's a tension, not a solution.

### Optimal IGF-1 Range

| IGF-1 Level | Assessment |
|------------|------------|
| <100 ng/mL | Too low — sarcopenia risk, poor recovery |
| 100–120 ng/mL | Low — monitor muscle mass, consider GH optimization |
| **120–200 ng/mL** | Optimal range — balances performance and longevity |
| 200–300 ng/mL | High-normal — good for muscle, monitor cancer screening |
| >300 ng/mL | High — potential concern for cancer risk |

### The Cycling Hypothesis

Attia has discussed cycling between periods of higher IGF-1 (training blocks with caloric surplus) and lower IGF-1 (Zone 2 focus, slight deficit, less protein emphasis). This may capture benefits of both states. Your current macrocycle structure — lean-out mesos followed by strength/hypertrophy mesos — naturally creates some of this oscillation.

### Practical Recommendations

- Test IGF-1 with baseline bloodwork
- If 120–200: you're in the sweet spot. No intervention needed
- If <120: optimize sleep, GH, and protein intake before considering sermorelin
- If >250: not an emergency, but prioritize cancer screening and consider periodic protein reduction
- Don't chase high IGF-1 for its own sake — the longevity data argues against it

## 9. Prolactin — The Overlooked Hormone

**Evidence: Moderate for clinical significance. Strong for the GnRH suppression mechanism.**

### Why It Matters

Prolactin is primarily known as a female lactation hormone, but in men it plays a role in immune function, reproductive health, and dopamine regulation. Chronically elevated prolactin (hyperprolactinemia) directly suppresses testosterone by inhibiting GnRH release from the hypothalamus — the same master signal that overtraining and cortisol disrupt.

### Normal Range

| Prolactin Level | Assessment |
|----------------|------------|
| 2–18 ng/mL | **Normal range for men** |
| 18–50 ng/mL | Mildly elevated — investigate causes |
| 50–100 ng/mL | Moderately elevated — medical evaluation needed |
| >250 ng/mL | Strongly suggests prolactinoma (pituitary tumor) |

### What Elevates Prolactin in Men

| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| **Stress** | HPA axis activation → prolactin release |
| **Sleep deprivation** | Disrupted dopamine regulation |
| **Medications** (antidepressants, antipsychotics, anti-nausea) | Dopamine antagonism |
| **Primary hypothyroidism** | Elevated TRH stimulates prolactin |
| **High estradiol** | Estrogen stimulates prolactin release |
| **Kidney/liver dysfunction** | Impaired prolactin clearance |
| **Intense exercise** | Acute transient spike (normalizes within hours) |

### Symptoms of Elevated Prolactin in Men

- Low libido
- Erectile dysfunction
- Fatigue / low motivation (dopamine suppression)
- Mood changes
- Reduced testosterone (via GnRH suppression)
- In extreme cases: galactorrhea (nipple discharge), visual field changes (pituitary mass effect)

### Natural Prolactin Management

| Approach | Mechanism | Evidence |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Vitamin B6 (P5P form, 50–100mg)** | Cofactor for dopamine synthesis (dopamine inhibits prolactin) | Moderate |
| **Zinc (15–30mg)** | Direct pituitary inhibition of prolactin | Moderate |
| **Vitamin E (400 IU)** | Protects dopamine neurons from oxidative stress | Weak-Moderate |
| **Ashwagandha** | May reduce prolactin via cortisol reduction | Moderate |
| **Adequate sleep** | Normalizes dopamine/prolactin rhythm | Strong |

> **Your current stack already covers zinc (15mg) and ashwagandha (600mg).** If prolactin comes back elevated on bloodwork, adding P5P (active B6) at 50mg is the first natural intervention. Vitamin B6 acts as a cofactor for aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC), the enzyme that synthesizes dopamine — and dopamine is the primary prolactin-inhibiting factor.

### When to Get Concerned

- Prolactin >25 ng/mL on a fasted morning draw (not post-exercise)
- Combined with low T, low libido, and fatigue
- Persistent elevation warrants pituitary MRI to rule out prolactinoma (almost always benign, treatable with cabergoline)

## 10. How All These Hormones Interact — The System View

The endocrine system is not a collection of independent dials. Every hormone in this document is connected to every other. Here's the integrated picture:

### The Steroid Hormone Pathway

```
Cholesterol
    ↓
Pregnenolone ──→ DHEA ──→ Androstenedione ──→ Testosterone
    ↓                                              ↓
Progesterone ──→ Cortisol                     Estradiol (via aromatase)
```

All steroid hormones — testosterone, estrogen, cortisol, DHEA — originate from cholesterol via pregnenolone. When the body is under chronic stress, cortisol production is prioritized. The "pregnenolone steal" theory (that cortisol production directly depletes the raw material for testosterone) has been challenged — the mechanism is more nuanced, involving enzyme regulation and cellular signaling rather than simple substrate competition. But the clinical observation holds: chronic stress → high cortisol → low testosterone. The pathway is through GnRH suppression, not raw material depletion.

### The Interconnected Web

| If This Is Off... | These Also Shift |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Cortisol high (chronic)** | T drops, thyroid slows, insulin resistance rises, GH suppressed, prolactin may rise |
| **Thyroid low** | Metabolism slows, T declines, SHBG changes, prolactin may rise (via TRH), weight gain |
| **Insulin high (resistance)** | SHBG drops → more free estradiol, inflammation rises, visceral fat increases → more aromatase |
| **Estradiol high** | T:E2 ratio off, prolactin rises, fat storage increases, feedback suppresses LH |
| **Sleep deprivation** | Cortisol rhythm breaks, GH drops 60–70%, T drops 10–15%, insulin sensitivity drops 25–40% |
| **Overtraining** | HPA axis blunts, all hormonal responses to exercise flatten, recovery collapses |
| **Body fat >20%** | Aromatase increases, estradiol rises, insulin resistance worsens, inflammation increases |

### The Positive Cascade

When the system is working:

```
Good sleep (8 hrs, cool, dark, consistent)
    ↓
Cortisol rhythm intact → morning peak, evening trough
    ↓
GH pulses strong during deep sleep → recovery, fat metabolism
    ↓
Testosterone production optimized → muscle, energy, libido
    ↓
Estradiol in balance (body fat 10-15%) → no excess aromatization
    ↓
Insulin sensitive (strength training + Zone 2) → lean, metabolically healthy
    ↓
Thyroid running optimally (adequate calories, selenium, zinc) → full metabolic rate
    ↓
DHEA-S adequate → adrenal reserve intact
    ↓
Training response strong → progressive overload → adaptation → repeat
```

> **The takeaway:** You don't fix hormones one at a time. You fix the foundations — sleep, training, nutrition, stress management — and the hormones follow. Your current protocol addresses most of these foundations. Bloodwork will reveal which specific hormones need targeted attention.

6. **2-week CGM experiment** — Dexcom Stelo, no prescription needed, ~$90. Wear through a normal training week to learn your actual glucose responses
