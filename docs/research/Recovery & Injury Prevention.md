## 1. Stability & Injury Prevention (Attia's Framework)

**Evidence: Strong for DNS principles. Moderate for specific implementation.**

Peter Attia organizes exercise into four pillars: **stability** (foundation), strength, aerobic efficiency (Zone 2), and anaerobic performance (Zone 5). Stability is the cornerstone — you cannot safely train the other three without it.

### What DNS Actually Is

Dynamic Neuromuscular Stabilization (DNS), developed by Professor Pavel Kolar, retrains the movement patterns we learned as infants. The core idea: if you can't breathe correctly, create intra-abdominal pressure, and control your spine simultaneously, you're building strength on a broken foundation.

DNS focuses on three capacities:
1. **Diaphragmatic breathing** — 360-degree expansion of the ribcage, not chest breathing
2. **Intra-abdominal pressure (IAP)** — bracing without breath-holding, the cylinder of stability around the spine
3. **Developmental positions** — supine, prone, quadruped, kneeling, standing — each training progressively more complex stabilization

### Attia's Practical Implementation

Attia recommends **1 hour of stability work per week**, split into 5-10 minute blocks done before other workouts. His go-to exercises:

| Exercise | What It Trains | How |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| Dead bugs | Anti-extension, IAP under load | Supine, opposite arm/leg extension while maintaining flat back |
| Planks / Stir the pot | Anti-extension endurance | Forearms on Swiss ball, small circles |
| Toe yoga | Foot/ankle stability, fall prevention | Independent toe lifting and spreading |
| Scapular CARs | Shoulder blade control | Controlled articular rotations through full range |
| Cat-cow | Spinal segmental control | Slow, deliberate vertebra-by-vertebra articulation |
| 90/90 hip switches | Hip internal/external rotation | Seated position changes with control |

### Cross-reference: What Chris Is Doing vs. What's Missing

**Already in the program** (from [Training Program](../../Volume%20Daddy/Training%20Program.md)):
- ATG prehab as warm-up on every session (external rotations, Powell raises, tib raises, Nordics, sissy squats)
- Back extensions (100 reps — addresses posterior chain endurance)
- Hanging leg raises (core finisher)

**Gap: No dedicated DNS/stability block.** The ATG warm-up covers joint preparation but not the foundational breathing and IAP patterns Attia emphasizes. A 5-minute DNS sequence before the ATG warm-up would address this.

**Recommended addition — Pre-warm-up DNS sequence (5 min):**
1. Crocodile breathing (prone, 10 breaths — learn diaphragmatic expansion against the floor)
2. Dead bugs (2x8 each side — IAP under contralateral load)
3. Bird dogs (2x8 each side — quadruped anti-rotation)
4. Toe yoga (1 min — foot stability, fall prevention)

> *Sources: [Attia — Stability as foundation](https://peterattiamd.com/peter-on-stability-the-foundation-of-the-4-exercise-components/) · [Attia podcast #152 — DNS with Michael Rintala](https://peterattiamd.com/michaelrintala/) · [Attia & Beth Lewis on DNS](https://peterattiamd.com/peter-attia-beth-lewis-on-dynamic-neuromuscular-stabilization-dns/) · [Attia & Huberman — 4 Pillars](https://www.mensjournal.com/health-fitness/peter-attia-andrew-huberman-4-pillars-longevity-fitness)*

## 3. Soft Tissue Work — What Actually Works

**Evidence: Moderate for foam rolling. Moderate for percussion. Weak for most recovery claims.**

### The Honest Assessment

| Modality | ROM/Flexibility | DOMS Reduction | Strength/Power | Recovery Speed |
|----------|----------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Foam rolling** | Moderate (+) | Small (+) | No effect | Small (+) for perceived recovery |
| **Percussion gun** | Moderate (+) | Small (+) | No effect / slight negative | No effect on lactate, RPE, contraction time |
| **Massage (hands-on)** | Small (+) | Small-Moderate (+) | No effect | Small (+) for perceived recovery |
| **Stretching (static)** | Strong (+) for passive ROM | No effect | Slight negative pre-workout | Not applicable |

### What the 2023 & 2025 Systematic Reviews Actually Found

**Percussion guns (Theragun, Hypervolt):**
- Effective for ROM: +5.4 degrees ankle dorsiflexion, +4.5-5.4 degrees on Thomas test
- Effective for reducing stiffness post-fatigue
- **NOT effective for strength or jump performance** — one study showed 3.1% reduction in jump height
- No differences in RPE, contraction time, or lactate vs. passive recovery
- Best used for flexibility/mobility prep, not performance enhancement

**Foam rolling:**
- Acutely positive for flexibility and ROM without impairing strength
- Favors perceived recovery and decreases DOMS
- Does not enhance maximal strength during activities

### Practical Protocol

| When | What | Duration | Frequency |
|------|------|----------|-----------|
| **Pre-workout** | Foam roll tight areas (quads, IT band, thoracic spine) | 60-90 sec per area | Every session |
| **Post-workout** | Percussion gun on worked muscles | 2+ min per muscle group, 20-40 Hz | As desired |
| **Off days** | Foam roll + percussion for perceived recovery | 10-15 min total | 2-3x/week |
| **DO NOT** | Percussion gun within 5 min of strength work | — | Reduces stiffness needed for force production |

**Bottom line:** Soft tissue tools help you feel better and move better. They do not accelerate actual tissue repair. Use them for warm-up prep and subjective recovery, not as a substitute for sleep and nutrition.

> *Sources: [Massage guns systematic review (PMC, 2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10532323/) · [Percussion therapy systematic review (IJSPT)](https://ijspt.scholasticahq.com/article/73795) · [Percussion vs. foam rolling for DOMS (PMC, 2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12286022/) · [Percussion and DOMS RCT (Frontiers, 2025)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2025.1561970/full)*

## 4. Whoop & Recovery Metrics

**Evidence: Moderate for HRV-guided training. Strong for trend analysis.**

### Which Metrics Actually Matter

| Metric | What It Tells You | Actionable? | How to Use |
|--------|------------------|-------------|-----------|
| **HRV (Heart Rate Variability)** | Autonomic nervous system balance — parasympathetic vs. sympathetic | Yes — strongest single metric | Track your personal baseline. Deviations of 10-15%+ below your rolling average signal incomplete recovery |
| **RHR (Resting Heart Rate)** | Cardiovascular stress, recovery status | Yes — especially trends | Elevated RHR + suppressed HRV for 2+ days = back off |
| **Recovery Score** | Composite of HRV, RHR, sleep, respiratory rate | Yes — simplified decision tool | Green (67-100%): push. Yellow (34-66%): maintain. Red (1-33%): active recovery only |
| **Sleep Performance** | Time in bed vs. sleep need, efficiency, disturbances | Yes — most controllable lever | Target 85%+ sleep efficiency. Below 80% = fix sleep hygiene before adding training load |
| **Strain** | Cardiovascular load during the day | Moderate — context-dependent | Match strain to recovery capacity. Don't chase high strain on yellow/red days |
| **Respiratory Rate** | Illness detection, stress | Early warning only | Sustained elevation (2+ breaths/min above baseline) = potential illness, extreme stress |

### Decision Framework

```
Morning Recovery Check:
├── Green (67-100%) → Train as programmed. Push for PRs if energy matches.
├── Yellow (34-66%) → Train, but:
│   ├── Drop 1 top set
│   ├── Cap RPE at 7-8
│   └── Add 1-2 min rest between heavy sets
└── Red (1-33%) → Options:
    ├── Light movement only (walk, yoga, mobility)
    ├── Swap to a deload-style session (50-60% loads, higher reps)
    └── Full rest if Red for 2+ consecutive days
```

### Key Insight: Trends > Single Days

A single red day means almost nothing — stress, poor sleep, alcohol, or even a particularly hard session can cause it. What matters is the **trend over 5-7 days**:

- HRV trending down over a week = accumulated fatigue, consider deload
- RHR trending up 5-10 BPM over multiple days = overtraining warning (cross-reference: [Testosterone Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md) overtraining signs)
- Sleep performance declining = root cause analysis before adding training volume

A 2020 study found runners who adjusted workouts based on Whoop recovery scores experienced fewer injuries and equal fitness gains compared to those who didn't — while spending less total time training.

> *Sources: [Whoop Recovery 101](https://www.whoop.com/us/en/thelocker/how-does-whoop-recovery-work-101/) · [HRV for training (Whoop)](https://www.whoop.com/us/en/thelocker/heart-rate-variability-training/) · [HRV Insights (Whoop Support)](https://support.whoop.com/s/article/Heart-Rate-Variability-HRV-Insights-WHOOP-Metrics)*

## 5. Deload Protocols

**Evidence: Moderate (expert consensus + survey data, limited RCTs).**

### How Often for a 40-Year-Old Intermediate Lifter

The 2023 International Delphi Consensus on deloading in strength sports found:

- Average deload frequency in competitive lifters: every **5.6 +/- 2.3 weeks**
- Average deload duration: **6.4 +/- 1.7 days** (roughly one week)
- 47.2% use proactive (pre-planned) deloads, 13.4% purely autoregulated, 39.4% hybrid

**For Chris at 39-40, rebuilding post-injury:**

| Approach | Frequency | Best For |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Every 4th week** (proactive) | 3 weeks on, 1 week deload | Conservative, safe for rebuilding. Matches [T Protocol Week 4](Testosterone%20Protocol.md) |
| **Every 5th-6th week** (hybrid) | 4-5 weeks on, 1 week deload | Once post-injury rebuild is complete and loads are heavier |
| **Autoregulated** (reactive) | When Whoop shows 3+ yellow/red days or strength declines 2+ sessions | Advanced — requires honest self-assessment |

### What to Do During a Deload

The Delphi consensus recommends reducing volume while optionally maintaining intensity:

| Variable | Deload Adjustment |
|----------|------------------|
| **Sets per exercise** | Reduce by 40-50% (e.g., 4 sets becomes 2) |
| **Reps per set** | Can maintain or reduce slightly |
| **Load (intensity)** | Option A: maintain weight, cut sets. Option B: reduce to 60-70%, maintain sets |
| **Frequency** | Keep the same — show up, just do less |
| **RPE cap** | Stay at RPE 6-7 max |
| **Accessories** | Cut in half or eliminate |

**Recommended deload for Chris (current phase):**
- Week 4 of every mesocycle
- Squat/DL: same movement, 70% of working weight, 2x5 (from 3x5/3x8)
- Upper lifts: drop top sets, keep backoff work at -10%
- ATG warm-up: full volume (prehab doesn't need deloading)
- Core finisher: maintain
- Cardio: Zone 2 only, no Zone 5

> *Sources: [Delphi consensus on deloading (PMC, 2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10511399/) · [Effects of deload on adaptations (PMC, 2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10809978/) · [Deloading survey — cross-sectional (PMC, 2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10948666/)*

## 7. Cold Exposure for Recovery

**Evidence: Strong for timing constraints. Moderate for recovery benefits. Strong against post-exercise CWI for hypertrophy.**

### The Critical Rule (Already Confirmed)

**Never cold plunge or cold shower within 4-6 hours after strength training.** A 2024 meta-analysis in the *European Journal of Sport Science* confirmed that post-exercise cold water immersion attenuates hypertrophy (though interestingly, not maximal strength). The mechanism: CWI suppresses muscle protein synthesis for up to 5 hours post-application by blunting anabolic signaling, reducing blood flow, and dampening the inflammatory response needed for adaptation.

Cross-reference: [Testosterone Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md) already states "Never cold plunge right after lifting — blunts hormonal response. Mornings only."

### Huberman's Protocol

Andrew Huberman recommends **11 minutes per week total** of deliberate cold exposure, split across 2-4 sessions of 1-5 minutes each.

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Temperature** | 50-59F (10-15C) for cold plunge. Cold showers are warmer but still beneficial |
| **Duration** | 1-5 min per session, 2-4 sessions/week |
| **Weekly total** | 11 min minimum for measurable benefits |
| **Timing** | Morning preferred. 6-8+ hours after lifting. Never immediately post-training |
| **Warm-up after** | Let the body reheat naturally — shivering is the mechanism for metabolic benefit |
| **Don't end warm** | Ending with a warm shower negates the norepinephrine and metabolic adaptation |

### Cold Showers vs. Cold Plunge

| Factor | Cold Shower | Cold Plunge |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Temperature** | ~60-70F (variable, hard to control) | 40-55F (controllable) |
| **Full body immersion** | No — head/neck warm, uneven exposure | Yes — full hydrostatic pressure |
| **Norepinephrine spike** | ~200% | ~200-300% |
| **Convenience** | High — no equipment | Low — requires tub, ice, or cold plunge unit |
| **Recovery benefit** | Moderate (discipline, mood, wake-up) | Moderate-Strong (more consistent dosing) |
| **Verdict** | Good enough for daily protocol | Better if accessible, not necessary |

### Chris's Current Protocol Assessment

The current cold shower protocol (Week 1: 30 sec, ramping to 3 min, morning only, not after lifting) is well-designed. The timing is correct for both testosterone optimization and not blunting training adaptations.

**If adding a cold plunge eventually:** Target 50-55F, 2-3 min, 3x/week mornings. Not after lifting. Budget options: chest freezer conversion ($200-400) or Morozko Forge / Plunge ($3,000-5,000+).

> *Sources: [Huberman — Cold exposure protocols](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/the-science-and-use-of-cold-exposure-for-health-and-performance) · [CWI and hypertrophy meta-analysis (EJSC, 2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11235606/) · [CWI blunts anabolic signaling (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4594298/) · [CWI blunts T response (PubMed)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31222379/) · [Rhonda Patrick — Cold stress hormesis](https://www.foundmyfitness.com/episodes/cold-stress-hormesis)*

## 8. Training Around Injuries at 40

**Evidence: Strong for RPE-based autoregulation. Moderate for specific pain management strategies.**

### The RPE Framework for Injury Management

Barbell Medicine's "Rating of Perceived Tolerance" (RPT) adds a pain dimension to standard RPE:

| RPT Level | What It Means | Action |
|-----------|--------------|--------|
| 1-3 | Mild discomfort, doesn't alter mechanics | Train through. This is normal adaptation stress |
| 4-6 | Moderate pain, some movement compensation | Reduce load 10-20%, focus on form. Acceptable to work through |
| 7-8 | Significant pain, movement altered to avoid it | Substitute movement. Find pain-free variation at same movement pattern |
| 9-10 | Severe pain, cannot perform movement | Stop. Rest. Reassess in 48-72 hours. Seek evaluation if persistent |

### RPE Ranges for Post-Injury Rebuilding

The current approach (70-80% effort on squat and deadlift) maps well to evidence-based recommendations:

| Training Phase | RPE Target | Reps in Reserve | Notes |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Rebuild (current)** | 6-7 | 3-4 RIR | Perfect for Chris now. Build volume before intensity |
| **Loading** | 7-8 | 2-3 RIR | Once weights feel comfortable and mechanics are locked |
| **Pushing** | 8-8.5 | 1-2 RIR | For competition lifts approaching targets (225 squat, 275 DL) |
| **Testing** | 9-9.5 | 0-1 RIR | Occasional singles or heavy triples. Not weekly |
| **Never** | 10 | 0 RIR (true failure) | Avoid on compounds for a 40-year-old. Reserve for isolation accessories only |

### When to Push Through vs. Back Off

**Push through (modify as needed):**
- Stiffness that loosens up after warm-up sets
- General muscle soreness (DOMS) from previous training
- "Old man morning" stiffness (joint creakiness that resolves with movement)
- Mild discomfort that stays constant (doesn't worsen) through the session

**Back off immediately:**
- Sharp, sudden pain during a rep
- Pain that worsens as the session continues
- Pain that alters your movement pattern (compensating)
- Joint pain with swelling
- Numbness, tingling, or radiating pain
- Any symptom that is new and unexpected

**The 48-hour rule:** If pain from training doesn't meaningfully improve within 48 hours, reduce training load by 20% next session and reassess. If it persists after a second reduced session, see a professional. The goal is improvement over weeks and months — symptoms will fluctuate, and the process is never linear.

### Practical Application for Current Rebuild

Chris's squat (185 3x5) and deadlift (185 3x8) are at conservative loads by design. The progression should be:

1. **Add reps before weight** — 185 3x5 becomes 185 3x6, 3x7, 3x8 before jumping to 195
2. **Backoff sets for volume** — match the upper body approach (heavy top sets + lighter backoff)
3. **Single-leg work as insurance** — Bulgarians and split squats are already in the program and build single-leg stability that protects during bilateral lifts
4. **Monitor session to session** — if recovery scores (Whoop) or perceived effort trend worse across 2+ weeks, deload regardless of schedule

> *Sources: [Rating of Perceived Tolerance (Barbell Rehab)](https://barbellrehab.com/rating-of-perceived-tolerance/) · [RPE and autoregulation (Barbell Medicine)](https://www.barbellmedicine.com/blog/autoregulation-and-rpe-part-i/) · [Autoregulation and injuries (EliteFTS)](https://elitefts.com/blogs/rehab-recovery/troubleshooting-strength-injuries-what-is-autoregulation) · [Velocity loss and RPE (SAGE, 2025)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/17479541251339905)*

## 9. Mobility vs. Flexibility — What a 40-Year-Old Needs

**Evidence: Strong for loaded mobility. Moderate for dedicated mobility work. Weak for static stretching as injury prevention.**

### The Key Distinction

- **Mobility** = active control through range of motion (what you can use under load)
- **Flexibility** = passive range of motion (what a stretch or gravity can achieve)
- **Stability** = the ability to control a position under force (Attia's foundation)

For a strength athlete at 40: **mobility > flexibility > isolated stretching.** You need ROM you can own under load, not ROM you can achieve passively.

### Resistance Training IS Mobility Work

A 2025 study in the *Journal of Strength & Conditioning Research* confirmed that resistance training through full range of motion improves joint flexibility comparably to stretching programs — and adds the strength to control that range. This aligns with the ATG philosophy: a deep squat is a mobility exercise.

Chris's program already includes significant loaded mobility:
- ATG split squats (full ROM knee/hip)
- Back squats to depth (hip/ankle mobility under load)
- Overhead press (shoulder flexion under load)
- Weighted chins (shoulder extension and scapular mobility under load)
- Nordics and sissy squats (knee flexion under eccentric load)

### What's Actually Missing

| Area | Why It Matters at 40 | Current Coverage | Recommendation |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Thoracic spine extension/rotation** | Stiffness here causes shoulder and low-back compensation | Minimal — no direct work | Add thoracic CARs or foam roll T-spine pre-workout |
| **Hip internal rotation** | Often lost first; causes knee/low-back issues in squats | Some from ATG split squats | Add 90/90 hip switches to warm-up |
| **Ankle dorsiflexion** | Limiting factor in squat depth | Tib raises + elevated split squats help | Consider wall ankle mobilizations |
| **Wrist extension** | Front rack position, push-ups, daily life | None | Add wrist CARs if pressing causes discomfort |

### Yoga Cross-Reference

From [Program Archive](Program%20Archive.md): Chris has a yoga practice (Vinyasa 2-3x/week, Yin 1x/week, 200-hour YTT in progress). This is excellent coverage for flexibility and active recovery. **Do not add more flexibility work.** The yoga practice + full-ROM resistance training + ATG prehab = comprehensive mobility programming.

**What yoga adds that the gym doesn't:**
- Sustained holds (Yin — fascia and connective tissue adaptation)
- Breath-movement coordination (supports DNS breathing patterns)
- Rotational patterns (twists — spinal health)
- Balance challenges (standing poses — proprioception)

## 10. Sleep & Recovery Quantification

**Evidence: Strong for sleep duration and consistency. Moderate for stage optimization.**

### Beyond 8 Hours — What Matters

Chris's 8-hour target is correct. The next optimization levers, in order of impact:

| Factor | Target | Why | How to Track |
|--------|--------|-----|-------------|
| **Consistency** | Same bed/wake time +/- 30 min, including weekends | Circadian rhythm entrainment. Irregular sleep = worse outcomes than short sleep in some studies | Whoop sleep consistency score |
| **Sleep efficiency** | 85%+ (time asleep / time in bed) | Below 80% = too much time awake in bed | Whoop |
| **Deep sleep** | 15-25% of total sleep (60-120 min for 8 hours) | Growth hormone release, tissue repair, immune function | Whoop — male average is 21% |
| **REM sleep** | 20-25% of total sleep | Cognitive recovery, emotional regulation, motor learning | Whoop |
| **Sleep latency** | Under 20 min | Falling asleep too fast (<5 min) = sleep debt. Too slow (>30 min) = arousal/anxiety | Whoop |

### Deep Sleep Optimization

Deep sleep is where growth hormone is released (75% of daily GH in first 1-2 hours of sleep — cross-reference [Testosterone Research](Testosterone%20Research.md)). It declines with age, making optimization critical at 40.

**What increases deep sleep:**

| Intervention | Evidence | Details |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| **Consistent wake time** | Strong | The single strongest driver of circadian rhythm. Anchor your wake time; bedtime follows |
| **Temperature** | Strong | Bedroom 65-68F. Core body temp must drop 2-3F for deep sleep onset. Already in [T Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md) |
| **Resistance training** | Moderate-Strong | Heavy compound training increases deep sleep percentage that night |
| **Magnesium glycinate** | Moderate | 400mg pre-bed — already in stack. Supports GABA receptor function |
| **No alcohol** | Strong | Even 1-2 drinks reduces deep sleep by 20-40%. Already eliminated in T Protocol |
| **No caffeine after noon** | Strong | Half-life is 5-6 hours. Already in protocol |
| **Glycine** | Moderate | 3-5g before bed lowers core temperature. In "considering" list in [Daily Routine](../Daily%20Routine.md) |

**What decreases deep sleep:**
- Alcohol (even small amounts)
- Late caffeine
- Irregular sleep schedule
- High ambient temperature
- Late heavy meals (stop eating by 7 PM — already in protocol)
- Blue light exposure (already managing with Kindle protocol)

### Whoop Sleep Metrics to Watch

1. **Sleep performance %** — Are you getting the sleep your body needs? Target 85%+
2. **Deep sleep duration** — Track your personal average, not population norms. Your own trend matters more
3. **Time in bed vs. asleep** — High discrepancy = sleep hygiene issue
4. **Disturbances** — Whoop tracks nighttime disruptions. If consistently high, investigate (temperature, noise, bathroom trips)

### Supplement Note

From [Daily Routine](../Daily%20Routine.md) "Considering" list: glycine (3-5g before bed) deserves promotion. A study showed it reduced sleep onset from 55 to 37 minutes and improved subjective sleep quality. At ~$10-20/month with no meaningful side effects, the risk-reward is favorable.

### The "Minimum Effective Off-Day"

If Chris adds nothing else, the current protocol (dog walk + meditation) covers the essentials. If optimizing:

**Ideal Off-Day Stack (45-60 min total):**
1. Morning cold shower (already in protocol)
2. Dog walk (already in protocol) — extend to 30-45 min if possible
3. 10-min foam rolling / mobility circuit (target tight areas from previous training day)
4. TM meditation (already in protocol)
5. Yoga on scheduled yoga days

### Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Walking — Emerging Option

Recent research shows BFR walking (2-4 mph, 15-20 min with cuffs) produces significant strength improvements even in trained athletes. This is a high-value active recovery modality — especially for Chris's lower body rebuild — but requires BFR cuffs ($30-150) and proper protocol education.

**BFR walking protocol:** 50-80% limb occlusion pressure, 2-4 mph, 4 sets of 5 min with 1 min rest between sets. Use on off days, not training days. This is supplementary, not a replacement for actual strength work.

### What NOT to Do on Off Days

- **No high-intensity cardio** — defeats the purpose of recovery
- **No "light" lifting sessions** that turn into real workouts — if you're at the gym, you'll push
- **No competitive sports** on planned recovery days — pickup basketball is not active recovery
- **No sitting all day** — sedentary off days are worse than light movement for recovery markers
