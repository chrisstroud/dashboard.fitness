Reference companion for dashboard.fitness. You can't optimize what you can't measure.

**Context:** 39-year-old man turning 40. Strength training 4x/week, cardio 2x/week, clean nutrition, full supplement stack. No baseline bloodwork yet. Wants to avoid TRT — natural optimization only. See [Testosterone Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md) and [Testosterone Research](Testosterone%20Research.md).

**Evidence key:** Strong = multiple RCTs or meta-analyses | Moderate = limited RCTs or observational | Weak = animal studies or single small trials

---

## The Philosophy: Medicine 3.0

Peter Attia's framework rejects "Medicine 2.0" — the model where you wait until something breaks, then treat it. Medicine 3.0 is proactive: detect problems 10-20 years before symptoms appear, intervene early, optimize toward the best outcomes rather than settling for "not sick."

**The core insight:** Lab reference ranges are population distributions. They tell you where you fall among a population that includes sick people. "Normal" includes the bottom 2.5% and top 2.5% — people already in trouble. Optimal ranges are where disease risk is lowest and function is highest.

> "Reference ranges are purely population-based distribution questions, literally just putting you up against a population distribution." — Peter Attia

**What this means for you:** Your first blood draw is the most important one. It establishes your personal baseline. Every draw after that tells a story about what's working, what's not, and what's changing with age.

## The Comprehensive Baseline Panel

Everything below should be drawn **once** to establish your baseline. Fasting, morning draw before 10 AM. The panel is organized by system.

### Cardiovascular & Lipids

**Evidence: Strong. Attia considers this the most actionable category for longevity.**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **ApoB** | Number of atherogenic particles in blood | Single best predictor of cardiovascular risk. Superior to LDL-C. Causal — not just correlated |
| **Lp(a)** | Genetically determined lipoprotein variant | Independent, causal risk factor for ASCVD. ~20% of people have elevated levels. One-time test — 70-90% genetic |
| **LDL-C** | Low-density lipoprotein cholesterol | Standard lipid marker. Useful but inferior to ApoB for risk stratification |
| **HDL-C** | High-density lipoprotein cholesterol | Higher is generally better. Context matters more than the number alone |
| **Triglycerides** | Blood fat level, fasting | Elevated TG = metabolic dysfunction signal. Ratio with HDL is informative |
| **Total Cholesterol** | Sum of all cholesterol fractions | Least useful on its own but part of the full picture |
| **ApoE Genotype** | Genetic variant (E2/E3/E4) | One-time test. E4 = elevated Alzheimer's and cardiovascular risk. Guides how aggressively to manage ApoB |

### Metabolic & Glucose

**Evidence: Strong. Attia calls insulin resistance "the most important metabolic derangement."**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Fasting Insulin** | Pancreatic insulin output at rest | The earliest warning of metabolic dysfunction — rises years before glucose does |
| **Fasting Glucose** | Blood sugar after 12+ hour fast | Basic metabolic screen. Can be misleadingly "normal" even with insulin resistance |
| **HbA1c** | 90-day average blood sugar | Good trend marker but can miss early dysfunction. Attia prefers OGTT |
| **HOMA-IR** | Calculated: (fasting insulin x fasting glucose) / 405 | Single best number for insulin resistance. Combines insulin and glucose into one metric |
| **OGTT with insulin** | 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test with insulin at 0, 30, 60, 120 min | Attia's gold standard. "Hyperinsulinemia on an OGTT, even in the presence of normoglycemia, is the canary in the coal mine" |

### Hormonal Panel

**Evidence: Strong for testosterone. Moderate for DHEA-S. See [Testosterone Protocol](Testosterone%20Protocol.md).**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Total Testosterone** | Total T in blood (bound + free) | Headline number. Must be morning draw before 10 AM. Two low draws required before considering intervention |
| **Free Testosterone** | Unbound, bioavailable T | The testosterone actually doing work. Can be low even with normal total T if SHBG is high |
| **SHBG** | Sex hormone-binding globulin | Binds 45-65% of testosterone. High SHBG = less free T available. Affected by thyroid, liver, insulin |
| **Estradiol (sensitive)** | Estrogen level via LC-MS/MS assay | Must request "sensitive" or LC-MS/MS — standard immunoassay is unreliable in men. Too high = aromatase activity from body fat |
| **DHEA-S** | Adrenal androgen precursor | Marker of adrenal reserve. Declines ~2-3% per year after 30. Low = adrenal fatigue signal |
| **LH / FSH** | Pituitary signaling hormones | Distinguish primary (testicular) vs. secondary (pituitary) hypogonadism. Critical if total T is low |

### Thyroid

**Evidence: Strong for TSH. Moderate for free T3/T4 in asymptomatic men.**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **TSH** | Thyroid-stimulating hormone | First-line thyroid screen. Subclinical hypothyroidism is common and underdiagnosed |
| **Free T3** | Active thyroid hormone | The hormone that actually drives metabolism. Low free T3 with normal TSH = poor conversion |
| **Free T4** | Thyroid prohormone | Converted to T3 in tissues. Context for the full thyroid picture |
| **TPO Antibodies** | Thyroid peroxidase antibodies | One-time baseline. Positive = Hashimoto's risk. Guides whether to monitor thyroid more aggressively |

### Inflammation

**Evidence: Strong for hsCRP. Moderate for homocysteine.**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **hsCRP** | High-sensitivity C-reactive protein | Systemic inflammation marker. Independent cardiovascular risk factor. Strength training lowers it over time |
| **Homocysteine** | Methylation and B-vitamin status | Elevated = cardiovascular, cognitive, and bone risk. Correctable with B vitamins (B6, B12, folate) |

### Nutrient Status

**Evidence: Strong for vitamin D. Moderate-Strong for Omega-3 Index. Moderate for ferritin, RBC Mg.**

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **25-hydroxyvitamin D** | Vitamin D blood level | Directly correlated with T levels, immune function, bone health. You're supplementing 5,000 IU — need to verify you're in range |
| **Ferritin** | Iron storage | Too low = fatigue, poor recovery. Too high = iron overload risk (hemochromatosis affects 1 in 200 men of European descent) |
| **RBC Magnesium** | Intracellular magnesium stores | Serum magnesium is nearly useless — only measures 1% of body stores. RBC mag reflects true tissue status over ~120 days |
| **Omega-3 Index** | EPA + DHA in red blood cell membranes | Rhonda Patrick's signature biomarker. Separate at-home test via OmegaQuant — not part of standard panels |
| **B12** | Vitamin B12 | Baseline check. Relevant for homocysteine metabolism and energy |

### Organ Function & Blood Counts

| Marker | What It Tells You | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **CBC with differential** | Complete blood count | Screens for anemia, infection, blood disorders. Baseline essential |
| **CMP** | Comprehensive metabolic panel | Liver enzymes (AST, ALT), kidney function (BUN, creatinine, eGFR), electrolytes |
| **GGT** | Gamma-glutamyl transferase | Liver health marker. Sensitive to alcohol, fatty liver, and metabolic stress |
| **Uric Acid** | Purine metabolism marker | Elevated = gout risk, cardiovascular risk, metabolic syndrome signal |

### Special Note: Creatine and Kidney Markers

**Evidence: Strong that creatine does NOT damage kidneys. Important to understand anyway.**

You take 10g creatine daily. This will likely elevate serum creatinine, which is used to estimate GFR (eGFR). A falsely low eGFR reading can alarm doctors who don't know you supplement. Meta-analyses confirm creatine supplementation does not alter actual kidney function — it just raises the input to the eGFR formula.

**What to do:** Tell your doctor you take creatine. If eGFR looks low, request cystatin C — an alternative kidney marker unaffected by creatine supplementation.

## ApoB, Lp(a), and Early Cardiovascular Risk

**Evidence: Strong. This is the single most important section for a 40-year-old man.**

### What a 40-year-old man should know

Cardiovascular disease is the #1 killer of men. It starts silently in your 20s-30s and manifests clinically in your 50s-60s. By the time you have symptoms, you have advanced disease. Attia's entire framework is built on the premise that early, aggressive lipid management can functionally eliminate ASCVD.

### ApoB: The Key Number

ApoB is a protein — one molecule per atherogenic lipoprotein particle. Measuring ApoB counts the actual particles that penetrate your arterial walls and cause atherosclerosis. LDL-C measures the cholesterol cargo inside those particles, which is a less direct measurement.

**Attia's position:** "Once you understand that ApoB is causally linked to ASCVD, to get into this discussion about managing 10-year risk makes no sense." His patients aim for < 60 mg/dL. His aspirational target is 20-40 mg/dL.

**What to do if your ApoB is elevated:**
1. Dietary intervention first: reduce saturated fat, increase fiber, reduce refined carbohydrates
2. If diet doesn't move it enough: low-dose statin or ezetimibe (Zetia) — Attia uses these readily
3. For aggressive lowering: PCSK9 inhibitors (injectable, highly effective)
4. Key insight: Attia treats early and aggressively. Waiting until 50 to address a high ApoB is 10 years of unnecessary arterial damage

### Lp(a): The Genetic Wild Card

Lp(a) is genetically determined (70-90%). You can't change it with diet or exercise. ~20% of the global population has elevated levels. It is the most prevalent hereditary risk factor for cardiovascular disease.

**Test it once.** That's all you need — it doesn't change over time.

**If elevated (> 75 nmol/L or > 30 mg/dL):**
- Manage ApoB, blood pressure, and metabolic health more aggressively than you otherwise would
- New therapies targeting Lp(a) directly (RNA-based drugs like pelacarsen and olpasiran) are in Phase III trials — expected approval 2026-2027
- Niacin modestly lowers Lp(a) but evidence for clinical benefit is weak
- PCSK9 inhibitors lower Lp(a) by ~20-30%

### ApoE Genotype: One-Time Genetic Test

Determines your E2/E3/E4 allele status. E4 carriers have higher baseline cardiovascular and Alzheimer's risk. Doesn't change — test once, ever.

**If E4 carrier:**
- More aggressive ApoB management
- Consider earlier and more frequent cognitive testing
- Prioritize sleep, exercise, and metabolic health even more intensely (all modifiable risk reducers)
- This is psychologically heavy information — some people prefer not to know. Attia tests all his patients.

## What Attia Gets Wrong (or What to Take with Context)

No framework is gospel. Some adjustments for your situation:

1. **OGTT is ideal but impractical for self-ordering.** Most direct-to-consumer labs don't offer the multi-point insulin OGTT Attia uses in his practice. Fasting insulin + fasting glucose + HbA1c together give you 80% of the signal. If all three are optimal, you're likely fine. If any are borderline, push for a full OGTT through your PCP.

2. **ApoB < 60 is aggressive.** Getting there without medication is rare. Most lean, active, clean-eating men land between 70-100. Below 80 is excellent. Below 60 typically requires pharmacological intervention. This is a personal risk-tolerance decision, not a mandate.

3. **Huberman tests twice per year.** Attia's patients test more frequently. For a new optimizer establishing baselines, starting with quarterly for hormones and metabolic markers, then settling to biannual, is the most practical cadence.

4. **InsideTracker's "optimal zones" are more conservative** than Attia's targets for ApoB and fasting insulin. Their software is great for tracking trends but don't treat their zones as the final word on optimal.
