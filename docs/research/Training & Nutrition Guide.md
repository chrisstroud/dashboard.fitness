# How to Train — Deep Research

Source: FoundMyFitness (Rhonda Patrick, Ph.D.). Distilled from interviews with Stuart Phillips, Brad Schoenfeld, Layne Norton, Martin Gibala, Benjamin Levine, Luc van Loon, Chris McGlory.

**Evidence key:** Strong = multiple RCTs or meta-analyses | Moderate = limited RCTs or observational | Weak = animal studies or single small trials

---

## Why It Matters

- After age 50, average person loses ~1% muscle mass and ~3% muscle strength per year
- Low muscle mass after 65 is linked to chronic disease, frailty, and mortality
- Stronger = better neurocognitive health and executive functioning
- VO2 max is one of the single best predictors of lifespan — up to 80% reduction in mortality risk for elite vs. lowest performers
- Low VO2 max is as bad or worse than coronary artery disease or smoking
- Increasing VO2 max by 3.5 ml/kg/min (1 MET) reduces all-cause mortality risk by 11%

## Resistance Training

### General Principles

> "The biggest lever you have is consistency with nutrition and training... It's not about being perfect. It's about showing up, doing the work, and being consistent over time." — Dr. Layne Norton

1. **Consistency** — the most effective program is one you can stick to
2. **Progressive overload** — gradually increase load, volume, and intensity
3. **Recovery** — adequate rest, nutrition, and sleep are vital
4. **Mindset and execution** — focus on execution over perfection

### Exercise Categories

**Pushing movements**
- Single-joint: chest fly, tricep extensions
- Multi-joint: bench press, overhead press, incline press, landmine press

**Pulling movements**
- Single-joint: bicep curls, concentration curls, preacher curls, reverse curls, face pulls
- Multi-joint: barbell/dumbbell row, lat pulldown, seated cable row, inverted row, bent-over row

**Leg movements**
- Single-joint: leg extensions, leg curls, calf raises
- Multi-joint: squats, leg press, deadlifts

**Accessory**
- Bicep curls, lateral raises, shrugs, planks, skull crushers

> "Multi-joint exercises are much more time-efficient than isolation exercises for hypertrophy, as they involve multiple muscle groups and stabilizers." — Dr. Brad Schoenfeld

---

### Training for Muscle Strength

**Evidence: Strong.**

Strength = force production via muscle size + nervous system efficiency. Strength loss (~3-4%/year with age) leads to functional impairment, poor balance, fall risk.

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Load & intensity | ≥ 85% 1RM, 1–5 reps per set |
| Volume | 2–3 sets per exercise per session; 5–10+ sets per exercise per week |
| Frequency | 1–2x per week per muscle group (more frequency → more gains, likely driven by volume) |
| Rest intervals | 2–5 minutes between sets |
| Exercise tempo | Fast (<1s concentric : <1s eccentric) to moderate (1–2s : 1–2s) |
| Exercise selection | Compound/multi-joint movements first |
| Exercise order | Multi-joint before single-joint |

---

### Training for Muscle Hypertrophy

**Evidence: Strong.**

Hypertrophy = increase in muscle fiber cross-sectional area. Driven by mechanical tension, muscle damage, and metabolic stress. Type II fibers especially vulnerable to age-related atrophy.

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Load & intensity | 60–80% 1RM, 6–12 reps per set |
| Volume | 3–6 sets per exercise per session; 10–20+ sets per exercise per week |
| Frequency | 2–3x per week per muscle group |
| Rest intervals | 1–2 minutes |
| Exercise tempo | Moderate (1–2s : 1–2s) to slow (2s : 4s) |
| Exercise selection | Mix of compound and isolation |
| Exercise order | Train priority muscles first; order doesn't significantly impact hypertrophy |

---

### Training for Body Recomposition

> "Recomping—gaining muscle while losing fat—is feasible, particularly for those who have a significant amount of weight to lose and are new to training." — Dr. Brad Schoenfeld

- Progressive resistance training, ≥ 3 sessions/week focused on hypertrophy
- Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg lean body mass (base on "ideal" body weight if overweight)
- Consume protein immediately post-workout
- Caloric deficit: 10–20% below maintenance
- **Sleep:** Poor sleep during calorie restriction → greater lean mass loss, less fat loss. Target 7–9 hours.

---

### Training to Failure

> "If your goal is just to build some muscle, gain some strength... training an hour a week, two half-hour sessions can give most people very nice results, provided you're training hard." — Dr. Brad Schoenfeld

- **For strength:** Stopping 3–4 reps before failure produces same strength gains without added recovery cost
- **For hypertrophy:** No evidence failure is superior to non-failure, but some utility exists
- **Best practice (Layne Norton):** Limit failure training to single-joint/machine exercises. Avoid failure on heavy compound movements. Everyone should train to failure at some point to learn what it feels like — beginners underestimate capacity by 5–6 reps.

---

### Time-Efficient Training

- 9 x 15-minute sessions/week = same strength gains as 3 x 45-minute sessions
- Train heavy (fewer reps, less time)
- Prioritize multi-joint exercises and bilateral training
- Cut rest intervals to 1–2 minutes
- Skip extensive warm-ups (a few light-load reps suffice)
- Skip stretching (no evidence it improves performance or reduces injury; may reduce strength/power output)

**Supersets:** Two exercises in succession, minimal rest. Can cut session length in half. Best for opposing muscle groups (e.g., biceps curl + triceps pushdown).

**Drop sets:** Perform a set, reduce load 20–25%, repeat. All sets to failure. Traditional scheme: 1–3 drops (2–4 total sets).

---

### Warm-Up & Stretching

- **Dynamic/specific warm-ups** may enhance heavy-load lifting performance
- **General warm-ups** (e.g., 10 min stationary bike) neither improve performance nor reduce injury — cut if time-limited
- **Stretching before resistance exercise reduces strength and power output**
- Resistance training itself increases range of motion to the same extent as passive stretching
- Only add stretching if mobility/flexibility is the primary session goal

## Aerobic Exercise Training

> "Three weeks of bed rest was worse for the body's ability to do physical work than 30 years of aging." — Dr. Benjamin Levine

### Intensity Matters More Than Duration

**Evidence: Strong.**

Recent studies show 1 minute of vigorous activity is worth far more than the WHO's assumed 2:1 ratio:

| Outcome | 1 min vigorous = X min moderate |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| All-cause mortality | 4.1 min |
| CVD mortality | 7.8 min |
| Cardiovascular events (MACE) | 5.4 min |
| Type 2 diabetes | 9.4 min |
| Cancer mortality | 3.5 min |

Compared to light activity, the ratios are even wider (53–156 min light = 1 min vigorous).

### Training Zones

| Zone | RPE (6–20) | Heart Rate | Talk Test |
|------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| 1 — Recovery | 6–8 | 50–60% HRmax | Easy conversation |
| 2 — Low/moderate | 9–12 | 60–70% HRmax | Full sentences with some effort |
| 3 — Threshold | 13–15 | 70–80% HRmax | Difficult to speak full sentence |
| 4 — High intensity | 16–18 | 80–95% HRmax | 1–2 words between breaths |
| 5 — VO2 max | 19–20 | 95%+ HRmax | Unable to speak |

**Max heart rate formulas** (all have ~10 bpm error):
- 220 − age
- 211 − (0.64 × age)
- 208 − (0.7 × age)

> "There's no magic to heart rate... Understanding your personal response to exercise and using perceived exertion can be more effective than relying on generic numbers." — Dr. Benjamin Levine

### High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

**Evidence: Strong.**

Benefits: greater VO2 max improvements in less than half the time vs. steady-state; 370% increase in muscle glucose transporters (diabetics); increased cardiac output and stroke volume; superior mitochondrial adaptations; increased autophagy; fast-twitch fiber recruitment; fat reduction via afterburn; elevated BDNF and cognitive function; eliminates non-responders (40% of people who see no improvement from standard endurance training).

#### Structuring HIIT

- **Work:rest ratios:** 1:1 (less intense) or 2:1 (higher intensity)
- **Work intervals:** ≥ 2 min most effective (VO2 max +3.5 ml/kg/min). Shorter intervals: +1–2 ml/kg/min.
- **Session volume:** ≥ 15 min most effective. < 5 min not very effective.
- **Training period:** 4–12+ weeks needed to see VO2 max improvements

#### HIIT Protocols

**Norwegian 4×4**
- 4 min at 85–95% HRmax, 3 min active recovery at ~70% HRmax, repeat 4x
- ~45 min total with warm-up/cooldown
- 7.2% VO2 max increase (men) / 4.2% (women) at 3x/week for 8 weeks
- Most people see improvements at 1x/week

**Tabata**
- 20 sec all-out, 10 sec rest, repeat 8x = 1 set
- 1–4 sets per workout, 3–5 min rest between sets
- ~25–45 min total
- VO2 max increases of 5–18% at 3–5 sets/week for 4–12 weeks

**10-Minute Workout (Gibala)**
- 10 x 60-sec intervals at 90% HRmax, 60 sec recovery between
- ~20 min total with warm-up
- 1–3x/week

---

### Endurance Training

- Intensity: 40–70% HRmax (RPE 6–12)
- Duration: 20–90+ min per session
- Frequency: 3–5x/week
- Volume: ≥ 150–300 min/week
- VO2 max improvement: ~2 ml/kg/min average

**Note on "Zone 2":** Commonly refers to lactate threshold training (~1.8–1.9 mM/L), not a heart rate zone. Correlating lactate zones with HR% is inherently imprecise.

---

### The Protocol That Reverses Cardiac Aging (Dr. Benjamin Levine)

**Evidence: Strong.** Older adults who performed this for 2 years reversed cardiac aging by almost 20 years.

- Heart starts shrinking at ~50, completes by ~65. After 70, virtually impossible to modify heart structure with training.
- Middle age = "sweet spot" for intervention

**The Protocol:**
1. **Volume:** 5–6 hours/week minimum
2. **HIIT:** Norwegian 4×4 or equivalent, 1x/week
3. **Recovery:** 20–30 min low-intensity aerobic the day after HIIT
4. **Endurance base:** 1-hour+ endurance session/week (talk test: can converse but not sing)
5. **Resistance training:** 2+ sessions/week

---

### VILPA & Exercise Snacks

**Evidence: Moderate-Strong.**

**VILPA** (Vigorous Intermittent Lifestyle Physical Activity): short bursts of high-effort activity in daily life (~10 sec to a few minutes). 4 min/day reduced all-cause, cancer, and CVD mortality by 26–34%. Up to 16 min/day: 43–55% reduction. Among self-identified non-exercisers.

**Exercise Snacks:** Brief isolated bouts of vigorous exercise throughout the day.
- 20-sec bike sprints every 1–4 hours (3 total): +6% VO2 max
- Vigorous stair climbing (60 steps) every 1–4 hours (3 total): +5% VO2 max
- 10 bodyweight squats every 45 min: +22% glucose regulation vs. sitting
- Optimal: 3–4x/day, ~4–5 min total. ~30 min/week total.

## Advanced Techniques

### Blood Flow Restriction (BFR)

> "BFR promotes similar muscle development to traditional resistance training, even with lighter loads." — Dr. Brad Schoenfeld

**Evidence: Moderate-Strong.**

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Frequency | 2–3x/week alongside heavy-load training |
| Occlusion pressure | 40–50% (arms), 60–80% (legs) |
| Exercises per session | 2 per muscle group (1 single-joint + 1 multi-joint) |
| Reps | 30-15-15-15 scheme or train to failure each set |
| Wear time | ≤ 10–20 minutes |
| Load | 20–50% 1RM |
| Tempo | 1–2s concentric : 1–2s eccentric |
| Rest | 30–60 seconds between sets |

- Keep cuff inflated during rest periods
- Perform BFR exercises after heavy-load work
- Schedule a 4-week BFR-free period periodically (limited long-term vascular data)
- Benefits: hypertrophy with lighter loads, 12% type I fiber growth, increased satellite cell content, useful for rehab/older adults

### Sauna

**Evidence: Moderate-Strong for recovery; Moderate for hypertrophy signaling.**

- Mimics exercise responses (hormesis). Sometimes called an "exercise mimetic"
- Growth hormone increase: 2–5x above baseline
- Elevates heat shock proteins
- Emerging evidence as countermeasure against age/disuse-related muscle atrophy

Combined with exercise:
- Improves heat tolerance and endurance performance
- Increases VO2 max
- Augments hypertrophic signaling (IL-6, HSPs, mTOR)
- Reduces DOMS, improves recovery

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Temperature | 80–90°C / 176–194°F |
| Duration | 15–30 minutes |
| Frequency | 1–3x/week beneficial; 4–7x/week for some outcomes |
| Timing | After exercise, ideally within 5–10 min of completion |

## Nutrition to Support Training

> "The most important aspect of protein consumption is meeting your total daily requirement." — Dr. Stuart Phillips

### Daily Protein Intake

| Population | Protein (g/kg body weight/day) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Sedentary or recreationally active | 0.8–1.2 |
| Active individuals and older adults | 1.2–1.6 |
| Athletes or very active | 1.6–2.2 |
| Bodybuilders or energy-restricted dieting | 2.2+ |

- RDA of 0.8 g/kg is a minimum, not optimal (Stuart Phillips)
- Sweet spot for training adaptations: **1.6 g/kg/day**
- Combining optimal protein + resistance training = greatest gains

### Protein Timing

> "The anabolic window is more like a barn door." — Dr. Brad Schoenfeld

- Total daily intake is far more important than timing
- Similar body composition, strength, and mass changes regardless of pre- vs. post-exercise protein or 3-hour delay
- **Exception for older adults (Luc van Loon):** Exercise before protein intake to counteract anabolic resistance. MPS is most robust 1–3 hours post-training.

### Protein Distribution

- Spread across multiple meals: ≥ 20–30g high-quality protein per meal
- Older adults: 20–40g per meal to maximize MPS
- Higher-protein breakfast (30g) reduces appetite, cravings, and reward-driven eating
- Even distribution is optimal but large single boluses (100g) are fully digested and absorbed (van Loon study)
- Practical recommendation: 3–4+ evenly spaced meals

## Supplements to Support Training

### Creatine

> "If I had to build my Mount Rushmore of supplements... creatine monohydrate, caffeine, whey protein... I just can't see an argument not to take it because of the cognitive benefits." — Dr. Layne Norton

**Evidence: Strong.**

Both Stuart Phillips and Layne Norton place creatine on their "tier 1" list.

**Benefits:**
- Body composition: increases fat-free mass, reduces body fat % even without training
- Strength & hypertrophy: significantly greater gains when combined with resistance training
- Older adults: enhanced strength, lean body mass, and functional performance vs. training alone
- Athletes: performance benefits for short bursts of high-intensity effort
- Bone health: 8+ g/day with resistance training may slow bone resorption (hip, spine)
- **Cognitive:** 5–10g/day increases brain creatine stores ~5–10%. Improvements in memory, processing speed, attention, reduced mental fatigue — especially under stress/sleep deprivation. Dietary creatine intake also associated with better cognitive scores.
- **Sleep:** Women supplementing 5g/day gained ~1 hour more sleep on exercise days (6-week trial)

**Dosing:**

| Goal | Dose |
|------|------|
| Strength & endurance | 5 g/day (saturates muscle in ~3 weeks) |
| Bone health | 8+ g/day |
| Cognitive performance | 20+ g/day (increases brain uptake; most studies use 5–10g) |

- No loading phase needed (3–5g/day for 28 days saturates stores)
- Take daily, not just on workout days
- Combine with carbohydrates (50–100g) or carbs + protein (50g) for enhanced muscle creatine retention
- Creatine monohydrate is the gold standard form

**Safety:**
- No evidence of hair loss (single unreplicated DHT study, didn't even measure hair)
- No evidence of kidney harm in healthy people (40+ years of data, over 1,000 studies)
- Even high doses for prolonged periods show no clinically relevant changes in renal function markers

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Training Context)

> "We've now seen a growing body of evidence that omega-3s are anabolic. They seem to mitigate declines in muscle mass and size, especially in older adults." — Dr. Chris McGlory

**Evidence: Moderate-Strong for anti-catabolic effects.**

See also: [Omega-3 Research](Omega-3%20Research.md) for supplementation quality, dosing, and safety.

**Training-specific benefits:**
- High-dose omega-3 (3g EPA + 2g DHA/day) during 2 weeks leg immobilization: nearly half as much muscle lost, full recovery during rehab
- Enhances muscle protein synthesis by improving insulin/amino acid response
- Anti-inflammatory effects may lessen sarcopenia severity
- Alters skeletal muscle membrane composition and mitochondrial bioenergetics
- Meta-analysis: omega-3 + resistance training improves lower-body strength and functional performance in older adults

**Dosing for muscle benefits (Chris McGlory):**

| Phase | Dose | Duration |
|-------|------|----------|
| Loading | 5g/day (3g EPA + 2g DHA) | 4 weeks (to change muscle phospholipid profile) |
| Maintenance | ~2g/day | Ongoing (to maintain Omega-3 Index ≥ 8%) |
